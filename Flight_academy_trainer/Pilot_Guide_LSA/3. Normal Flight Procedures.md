## 3. Normal Flight Procedures



- **What is the purpose of normal procedures?**

  > "The purpose of normal procedures is to establish a standardized, safe, and consistent method for operating the aircraft. Following normal procedures ensures that no critical steps are forgotten during any phase of flight. For example, using a checklist before take-off confirms that the aircraft configuration is correct. Ultimately, disciplined adherence to normal procedures minimizes human error and maximizes flight safety."  

- **What should a pilot check during pre-flight inspection?**

  > "During a pre-flight inspection, the pilot must thoroughly check the structural integrity and vital fluids of the aircraft. This includes inspecting fuel, oil, and coolant levels, as well as the condition of the controls, tires, brakes, propeller, and pitot tube. For example, checking the fuel sumps ensures there is no water contamination. A meticulous walk-around is the first line of defense against mechanical emergencies in the air."  

- **Why is fuel quantity important before flight?**

  > "Fuel quantity is critical because the aircraft must have enough usable fuel to safely reach its destination, plus a regulatory safety reserve. Running out of fuel causes complete engine starvation, resulting in a forced landing. For instance, unexpected headwinds can increase fuel burn during a cross-country flight. Verifying precise fuel levels before departure ensures the flight can be completed without the risk of fuel exhaustion."  

- **What should the pilot check before take-off?**

  > "Before entering the runway, the pilot must verify that the aircraft is fully configured for departure. This means checking the altimeter setting, trim position, flight controls, canopy lock, safety harnesses, fuel selector, flaps, and instruments. For example, an unlatched canopy can open in flight and cause severe distraction. Completing the pre-take-off checklist guarantees that the aircraft is safe and ready to become airborne."  

- **What should the pilot monitor during take-off?**

  > "During the take-off roll, the pilot must continuously monitor engine power, airspeed acceleration, and primary gauges. The pilot needs to ensure the engine achieves full RPM and that the airspeed indicator is alive and rising. If any parameter is abnormal, such as sluggish acceleration, the take-off must be aborted immediately. Continuous monitoring during this high-risk phase ensures a safe liftoff or a timely rejected take-off."  

- **What is important during climb?**

  > "During the climb phase, it is vital to maintain the correct target climb airspeed ($V_Y$ or $V_X$) while monitoring engine temperatures. Since the aircraft is operating at high power and lower speeds, engine cooling is reduced. For example, a pilot may need to lower the nose slightly to increase airflow and lower high oil temperatures. Managing speed and engine gauges during climb prevents engine overheating and maintains a safe margin above stall."  

- **What does the pilot do during cruise?**

  > "During the cruise phase, the pilot transitions the aircraft to a stable flight profile by setting cruise power, trimming the aircraft, and monitoring fuel consumption. The pilot must also track their position, monitor weather conditions, and maintain radio contact with ATC. For safety reasons, continuous situational awareness during cruise ensures that the flight remains on course and any changes can be handled calmly."  

- **What should the pilot check before landing?**

  > "Before entering the airport traffic pattern, the pilot must perform a pre-landing checklist. This includes confirming the approach speed, setting the propeller control, turning on fuel pumps, setting landing flaps, and adjusting trim. For instance, extending flaps lowers the touchdown speed and stabilizes the approach. Proper cockpit configuration before landing reduces pilot workload and ensures a stabilized, safe touchdown."  

- **What is a go-around?**

  > "A go-around is a standard safety maneuver where a pilot rejects a landing and transitions back into a climb to try the approach again. It involves applying full engine power, retracting flaps incrementally, and establishing a positive rate of climb. For example, if wind shear destabilizes the aircraft on short final, a go-around is executed. Initiating a go-around is a normal safety procedure, not a failure."  

- **When should a pilot decide to go around?**

  > "A pilot should decide to go around anytime the approach becomes unstable, if there is traffic on the runway, or if sudden gusts compromise control. Trying to force a bad approach into a landing often leads to hard touchdowns or runway excursions. For example, if the aircraft bounces severely on the runway, a go-around should be performed immediately. Prioritizing a go-around over an unsafe landing is a sign of good pilot decision-making."
