## 4. Emergency Procedures



- **What is the first priority in any emergency?**

  > "The absolute first priority in any emergency is to maintain control of the aircraft. The primary risk of a distraction or panic during a technical issue is a loss of control or an aerodynamic stall. The pilot must strictly follow the classic rule: **Aviate, Navigate, Communicate**. Only after the plane is stable and flying at a safe airspeed should the pilot talk to ATC. Keeping the aircraft flying safely remains the ultimate priority."  

- **What should the pilot do after engine failure during take-off?**

  > "The situation is an engine failure immediately after take-off, which carries a critical risk due to low altitude and airspeed. The pilot must immediately lower the nose to maintain safe gliding speed and prevent an aerodynamic stall. If altitude is insufficient to return, the pilot must land straight ahead, avoiding sharp turns. If time permits, declare a Mayday to ATC. The ultimate goal is to land under control with minimum risk."  

- **Why is it dangerous to turn back to the runway at low altitude?**

  > "This situation is an engine failure on initial climb, and the primary risk is known as the 'impossible turn.' Turning back at low altitude requires a steep bank angle, which dramatically increases the stall speed and causes a rapid loss of altitude. Instead of returning safely, the aircraft may spin and crash. The pilot must maintain control and land ahead, as securing a controlled touchdown is much safer than risking an aerodynamic stall."  

- **How should the pilot choose an emergency landing area?**

  > "When facing a forced landing, the pilot must quickly select an emergency landing area to minimize the risk of a severe impact. The main risks are colliding with power lines, trees, or flipping over on rough terrain. The pilot should prioritize large, flat open fields, aiming to land into the wind to minimize ground speed. Broadcast the chosen intentions to ATC if possible. Choosing the clearest available area maximizes occupant survival."  

- **What is the difference between emergency landing and precautionary landing?**

  > "An emergency landing is caused by an immediate, critical threat like an engine failure, while a precautionary landing is an intentional decision made before a situation turns into an emergency. The risk of delaying a precautionary landing—such as during deteriorating weather or low fuel—is that options disappear. The pilot should execute a precautionary landing while the aircraft is still fully controllable. Making a timely, proactive landing is always the safest choice."  

- **What should the pilot do in case of fire in flight?**

  > "An in-flight fire is a life-threatening emergency because smoke can quickly incapacitate the crew and damage controls. The pilot must immediately reduce the source of the fire by turning off the fuel selector or shutting down electrical systems, then establish a steep descent to land as soon as possible. Simultaneously, declare an emergency to ATC to request rescue services. Landing quickly is crucial to saving the aircraft and passengers."  

- **What should the pilot do if smoke appears in the cockpit?**

  > "The presence of smoke in the cockpit indicates an electrical or engine fire, creating an immediate risk of toxic inhalation and loss of visibility. The pilot should turn off the master switch to isolate electrical components, open fresh air vents, and use an emergency oxygen mask or smoke goggles if available. Inform ATC of an immediate emergency and descend for an emergency landing. Swift action prevents cabin incapacitation."  

- **What should the pilot do if the autopilot malfunctions?**

  > "An autopilot malfunction is an abnormal situation that can cause uncommanded aircraft attitudes or deviations from assigned altitudes. The risk is that the automation could force the aircraft into an overstressed state or an unexpected dive. The pilot must immediately disconnect the autopilot using the disconnect button or pull the circuit breaker, and fly the aircraft manually. Inform ATC of the system loss, as pilot manual control is the only way to ensure flight path safety."  

- **What should the pilot do in icing conditions?**

  > "Encountering unexpected icing is a critical hazard because it changes the wing profile, destroys lift, and increases drag. The risk is a severe aerodynamic stall at a higher airspeed than normal. The pilot must exit the icing layer immediately by turning 180 degrees back or changing altitude to find warmer air. Notify ATC of the icing encounter and request vectors out of the area. Prompt avoidance is necessary to prevent a loss of control."  

- **When should the pilot communicate with ATC during an emergency?**

  > "A pilot should communicate with ATC only after the aircraft is under complete manual control and immediate safety is secured. The risk of communicating too early is that talking distracts the pilot from flying the plane during a critical moment. Pilots must adhere to the **Aviate, Navigate, Communicate** order of priorities. Once stable, declare 'Mayday' or 'Pan-Pan' to receive priority handling. Safe aircraft management must always come before radio work."
