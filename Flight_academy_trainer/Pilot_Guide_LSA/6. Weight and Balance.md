## 6. Weight and Balance



- **What is weight and balance?**

  > "Weight and balance is the mathematical calculation of the aircraft's total weight and its center of gravity (CG) location before flight. This ensures the plane is within safe structural and aerodynamic design limits. For example, an incorrect weight distribution changes how the flight controls feel. Performing this calculation before flight ensures that the aircraft remains stable and controllable throughout the journey."  

- **Why must the pilot check aircraft weight before flight?**

  > "The pilot must check the total weight to ensure the aircraft does not exceed its maximum structural limits, such as the maximum take-off weight. An overweight aircraft suffers from degraded structural safety margins and poor climb capability. For instance, it may fail to climb out of ground effect safely. Verifying the total payload is a critical pre-flight step to guarantee aircraft performance."  

- **What is maximum take-off weight?**

  > "Maximum take-off weight (MTOW) is the absolute highest weight certified by the manufacturer for the start of the take-off roll. This limit is based on structural strength and climb performance requirements. For example, operating at MTOW requires a longer runway but is perfectly safe if properly planned. Keeping the aircraft at or below MTOW ensures that all safety margins remain intact."  

- **What items must be included in loading calculation?**

  > "The loading calculation must include the aircraft's basic empty weight, the weight of the pilot and passengers, all fuel onboard, and any baggage stored in the compartments. Skipping any of these weights will result in an inaccurate center of gravity calculation. For example, heavy baggage placed far back can push the plane out of balance. Complete and accurate logging of all payload items is required for flight safety."  

- **What can happen if the aircraft is overloaded?**

  > "An overloaded aircraft faces a high risk of structural deformation and severely degraded flight characteristics. It will need an extended runway to take off, experience a dangerously high stall speed, and climb very slowly. For instance, it might be unable to maintain altitude if an engine issue occurs. To ensure safety, a pilot must remove fuel or baggage if the total weight exceeds limits."  

- **What is center of gravity?**

  > "The center of gravity, or CG, is the imaginary point along the aircraft airframe where the total weight is perfectly balanced. The CG must remain within specific forward and aft limits for the flight controls to work effectively. For example, if the CG moves outside the approved range, the aircraft can become unstable. Keeping the CG within limits ensures stable handling and predictable aerodynamics."  

- **Why can a forward CG be dangerous?**

  > "A forward center of gravity is dangerous because it makes the aircraft nose-heavy, increasing the aerodynamic load on the tail. This condition requires more back-pressure on the stick, making it difficult to raise the nose during take-off or landing flare. For example, a severe forward CG can lead to a nose-wheel first touchdown and propeller strike. Proper balance distribution prevents control authority issues during landing."  

- **Why can an aft CG be dangerous?**

  > "An aft center of gravity is extremely dangerous because it reduces the aircraft's longitudinal stability, making it tail-heavy. In this condition, the aircraft can easily enter an uncommanded pitch-up attitude, and recovering from an aerodynamic stall or spin can become impossible. For instance, a tail-heavy plane might pitch up dangerously during take-off. Maintaining a safe CG margin prevents loss-of-control accidents."  

- **Why must baggage be secured?**

  > "Baggage must be securely strapped down because any loose items can shift during acceleration, turbulence, or steep turns. If heavy bags shift rearward, they can instantly move the center of gravity outside safe limits, causing severe control instability. For example, loose cargo can block or damage control cables. Securing all baggage before engine start removes the risk of unexpected balance shifts."  

- **What would you say to a passenger who wants to take too much baggage?**

  > "I would professionally explain to the passenger that baggage capacity is strictly limited by aircraft structural weight, balance envelopes, and aviation safety laws. I would show them that an extra bag could compromise our takeoff performance and endanger our lives. For safety reasons, I would refuse to carry excess baggage that puts the aircraft over its maximum limit, as safety is always the first priority."
