## 7. Aircraft Systems and Equipment

- **What are the main aircraft systems?**

  > "The main systems of the Bristell LSA include the flight control system, the fuel system, the electrical system, the pitot-static system, the brakes, the digital avionics, and emergency gear like the ELT. Each system is designed to perform a distinct function to keep the aircraft operational. For example, the electrical system powers our cockpit screens. A complete understanding of these systems allows the pilot to diagnose and respond to any faults early."  

- **What does the control system do?**

  > "The flight control system connects the pilot's inputs in the cockpit to the aerodynamic surfaces on the wings and tail. This allows the pilot to manage the aircraft's pitch, roll, and yaw attitudes smoothly. For example, moving the control stick translates into immediate aircraft movement. Regular pre-flight checks of the control cables ensure that the system operates freely and without any restrictions."  

- **What is the function of the elevator?**

  > "The elevator is a primary control surface located on the horizontal stabilizer that controls the aircraft's pitch axis. When the pilot pulls back on the control stick, the elevator moves up, forcing the tail down and causing the aircraft nose to pitch upward. For safety, the elevator must remain free of ice and damage to guarantee that the pilot always maintains pitch control."  

- **What is the function of the ailerons?**

  > "The ailerons are primary control surfaces positioned on the outer trailing edges of the wings that control the aircraft's roll axis. Deflecting the control stick left or right moves the ailerons differentially, causing the aircraft to bank into a turn. For example, precise aileron control is necessary to counter crosswinds during landing. Verifying full aileron deflection during the pre-flight check ensures proper roll authority."  

- **What is the function of the rudder?**

  > "The rudder is a vertical control surface located on the tail assembly that manages the aircraft's yaw axis. Controlled by the pilot's feet via the rudder pedals, it aligns the aircraft nose during turns and maintains tracking on the runway. For example, rudder input is essential to keep the aircraft coordinated during steep turns. Proper use of the rudder prevents uncoordinated flight and aerodynamic stalls."  

- **Why are flaps important during take-off and landing?**

  > "Flaps are secondary control surfaces that alter the wing shape to increase both lift and aerodynamic drag. During take-off, a small flap setting shortens the ground roll, while during landing, full flaps allow for a steeper approach and a lower touchdown speed. For instance, using landing flaps allows the pilot to stop safely on a short runway. Correct flap management maximizes structural safety margins during low-speed flight."  

- **What does the fuel system do?**

  > "The fuel system stores aviation fuel safely within the wing tanks and delivers a clean, pressurized supply to the engine. It includes tank selectors, fuel lines, filters, and both mechanical and electrical fuel pumps. For example, switching tanks ensures balanced fuel consumption. Monitoring fuel pressure and quantities continuously ensures that the powerplant runs smoothly without starvation hazards."  

- **Why is the electrical system important?**

  > "The electrical system is critical because it generates, stores, and distributes electrical power to the aircraft's avionics, instruments, fuel pumps, and lights. It consists of an engine alternator, a battery, master switches, and protective circuit breakers. For instance, if the alternator fails, the battery provides a limited back-up supply. Monitoring the voltmeter ensures that the pilot has sufficient power to run essential flight gear."  

- **What instruments use the pitot-static system?**

  > "The pitot-static system provides air pressure data to primary flight instruments, specifically the airspeed indicator and the altimeter. The pitot tube captures dynamic ram air pressure, while the static ports measure ambient atmospheric pressure. For safety, the pilot must ensure that the pitot cover is removed before flight so that these instruments display accurate information."  

- **What can happen if the pitot tube is blocked?**

  > "If the pitot tube becomes blocked by ice or insects, the airspeed indicator will display incorrect and dangerous information. It may show a constant zero or behave like an altimeter, rising during a climb and falling during a descent. This can easily lead to an accidental aerodynamic stall if the pilot relies on false readings. Switching on the pitot heat or cross-checking with GPS ground speed helps ensure safety."  

- **What is the function of the transponder?**

  > "The transponder is an airborne radio transmitter that replies to radar signals from air traffic control. It transmits the aircraft's discrete squawk code, position, and precise altitude information directly to the controller's radar screen. For example, setting squawk 7700 alerts ATC to an emergency. An active transponder improves situational awareness and maintains safe aircraft separation."  

- **What is the function of the ELT?**

  > "The Emergency Locator Transmitter, or ELT, is a safety device designed to activate automatically during a hard impact or crash. Once triggered, it broadcasts distress signals on emergency frequencies ($121.5\text{ MHz}$ and $406\text{ MHz}$) to assist search and rescue teams in locating the aircraft site. Ensuring the ELT is armed before flight provides a vital safety net in the event of an accident."  

- **Why should a pilot be ready to fly manually if the autopilot fails?**

  > "A pilot must always maintain manual flying skills because automation can fail unexpectedly due to electrical or software glitches. If the autopilot disconnects in poor weather, a pilot who relies too much on automation might experience spatial disorientation or lose control. For example, sudden uncommanded inputs require immediate pilot intervention. Regular manual flying practice ensures that the pilot remains the ultimate authority over the flight path."
