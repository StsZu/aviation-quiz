## ICAO Picture Skeleton — Crosswind Landing / Unstable Approach

### 1. General situation

- large passenger aircraft on final approach
- landing phase
- runway visible ahead
- aircraft slightly banked / not perfectly aligned
- possible crosswind landing
- demanding but normal landing situation

### 2. What I can see

- wide-body aircraft from behind
- landing gear extended
- flaps extended
- runway lights and markings
- aircraft close to touchdown
- urban area near the airport
- possible haze / reduced visibility
- aircraft wings not level

### 3. What may be happening

- aircraft landing in crosswind conditions
- pilot correcting drift
- aircraft crabbing or banking into the wind
- final approach close to runway threshold
- possible unstable approach if alignment is poor
- crew preparing for touchdown or go-around

### 4. Possible causes

- crosswind from the side
- gusty wind
- turbulence near the runway
- wake turbulence from previous traffic
- wind shear possibility
- late correction on final approach
- unstable approach speed or alignment

### 5. Main risks

- hard landing
- runway excursion
- wingtip or engine nacelle strike
- bounced landing
- loss of directional control after touchdown
- unstable approach continued too long
- go-around required at low altitude

### 6. What the crew should do

**Pilot flying:**

- maintain correct approach speed
- keep runway centerline
- correct drift
- use proper crosswind technique
- avoid excessive bank angle
- be ready to go around

**Pilot monitoring:**

- monitor speed, sink rate, alignment
- call out deviations
- support decision-making
- announce unstable approach if needed

**ATC:**

- provide wind information
- report windshear or gusts if known
- clear runway area
- expect possible go-around

### 7. Safety conclusion

- stable approach is essential
- if the aircraft is not stabilized, go around
- crosswind landing requires good control and timely corrections
- landing should not be forced
- safety is more important than saving one approach

## Short speaking frame

Use this order:

1. **I can see** → aircraft landing, gear down, flaps extended
2. **It may be** → crosswind landing / unstable approach
3. **Possible causes** → side wind, gusts, turbulence
4. **Main risk** → hard landing, runway excursion, loss of control
5. **Action** → maintain centerline, monitor speed, go around if unstable
6. **Conclusion** → stable approach first, landing second

# ICAO Story — Level 4 Style

In this picture, I can see a large passenger aircraft on final approach to the runway. The aircraft is configured for landing, with the landing gear extended and the flaps deployed. The aircraft appears slightly banked, which suggests that the crew may be dealing with crosswind conditions.

This may be a normal crosswind landing, but it could also become an unstable approach if the aircraft is not correctly aligned with the runway. Possible contributing factors include gusty wind, turbulence near the runway, wake turbulence from previous traffic, or a late correction by the pilot.

From a safety perspective, the main risks are hard landing, bounced landing, runway excursion, or loss of directional control during the landing roll. If the aircraft touches down with too much side drift or excessive bank angle, the landing gear may be stressed, and the aircraft may not remain on the centerline.

The pilot flying should maintain the correct approach speed, control the drift, and keep the aircraft aligned with the runway centerline. The pilot monitoring should call out any deviation in speed, sink rate, or alignment. ATC should provide accurate wind information, especially if there are gusts or wind shear reports.

In my opinion, the key decision in this situation is not only how to land, but also when to stop trying. If the approach is not stabilized, the crew should go around without hesitation.

# ICAO Story — Level 5 Style

This picture shows a large commercial aircraft in the final stage of approach, very close to the runway. The aircraft is fully configured for landing, with the landing gear down and the flaps extended. Its attitude suggests that the crew may be correcting for crosswind or gusty conditions.

At first glance, this could be a normal crosswind landing. However, from an examiner’s point of view, it is worth discussing the stability of the approach. The aircraft seems to be close to touchdown, so any significant deviation in speed, sink rate, bank angle, or runway alignment would leave very little time for correction. In crosswind conditions, the aircraft may drift sideways, and the pilot has to manage both alignment and descent rate at the same time.

The possible causes of this situation include strong side wind, gusts, mechanical turbulence near buildings, or a late correction on short final. Wake turbulence from a preceding aircraft could also be a factor, although we cannot confirm it from the picture. The weather does not look severe, but the background appears slightly hazy, and wind can be dangerous even when visibility is acceptable.

The main operational risk is that the crew may continue an unstable approach too long. A hard touchdown, bounced landing, runway excursion, or loss of directional control can happen very quickly during the flare and landing roll. A wingtip strike or engine nacelle strike is also possible if the bank angle becomes excessive close to the ground.

The correct response is disciplined monitoring and early decision-making. The pilot flying should maintain the correct speed, control the aircraft’s drift, and align it with the runway before touchdown. The pilot monitoring should actively call out deviations, not just observe silently. If the aircraft is not stabilized by the company’s required altitude, the crew should initiate a go-around.

In my opinion, this picture shows one of the most important lessons in airline operations: a landing is optional, but a go-around is a normal safety procedure. Pilots should not try to “save” a poor approach at low altitude. That is how small deviations become serious incidents.

**Safety conclusion:** in crosswind conditions, the crew must protect the stable approach criteria. If speed, alignment, or descent rate is not safe, the best decision is to go around and set up for another approach.
