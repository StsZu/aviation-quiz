## ICAO Picture Skeleton — Engine / Ground Vehicle Collision

### 1. General situation

- aircraft on apron / stand area
- ground support vehicle very close to engine
- engine nacelle damaged
- possible collision during ground handling
- abnormal ground operation situation

### 2. What I can see

- commercial passenger aircraft
- orange engine nacelle
- damaged engine cowling / nacelle
- ground tug / pushback vehicle
- wet apron surface
- aircraft fuselage and wing section
- ground handling company vehicle
- aircraft parked or being moved

### 3. What may be happening

- ground vehicle hit the engine
- aircraft may have been parked at the gate
- tug may have approached too close
- possible pushback or towing operation
- possible ground handling incident
- aircraft probably not airworthy after impact

### 4. Possible causes

- poor communication between ground crew and cockpit
- incorrect marshalling
- driver misjudged distance
- low visibility / wet surface
- lack of situational awareness
- vehicle brake or control problem
- non-compliance with safety procedures
- working under time pressure

### 5. Main risks

- engine damage
- hidden structural damage
- fuel or oil leakage
- fire risk
- aircraft unfit for flight
- injury to ground staff
- expensive maintenance delay
- unsafe departure if damage is missed

### 6. What the crew / ground staff should do

**Flight crew:**

- stop the operation
- set parking brake
- shut down engine if running
- inform ATC / ground control
- request inspection and maintenance
- do not depart until aircraft is cleared

**Ground crew:**

- stop vehicle immediately
- secure the area
- report the incident
- keep personnel away from engine
- check for leaks or fire risk
- follow company emergency procedures

**Maintenance:**

- inspect engine nacelle
- check fan blades and internal parts
- check for hidden damage
- decide if aircraft is airworthy

### 7. Safety conclusion

- aircraft must not continue flight without inspection
- ground operations require clear communication
- safe distance from aircraft engines is critical
- better to delay the flight than risk unsafe departure
- safety first, schedule second — the boring rule that saves very expensive metal

## Short speaking frame

Use this order:

1. **I can see** → aircraft engine + ground vehicle + damage
2. **It may be** → ground collision during towing / pushback / apron operation
3. **Possible causes** → poor communication / misjudged distance / wet surface
4. **Main risk** → engine damage / fire / aircraft not airworthy
5. **Action** → stop operation / secure area / inspect aircraft
6. **Conclusion** → do not fly until maintenance clears the aircraft

# ICAO Story — Level 4 Style

In this picture, I can see a commercial aircraft on the apron with visible damage to the engine nacelle. A ground support vehicle, probably a tug or pushback vehicle, is positioned very close to the damaged engine.

This appears to be a ground collision during apron operations. The vehicle may have approached the aircraft too closely, or the driver may have misjudged the distance. Another possible cause is poor communication between the ground crew and the cockpit. The wet apron surface could also have increased the risk because the vehicle may have had reduced braking performance.

From a safety perspective, this is a serious situation. The visible damage to the nacelle may indicate deeper damage inside the engine. There could be damage to the fan blades, sensors, engine mounts, or surrounding structure. There is also a possible risk of fuel or oil leakage.

The correct action is to stop all movement immediately. The flight crew should set the parking brake, shut down the engine if necessary, and inform ground control or ATC. The ground crew should secure the area, report the incident, and keep people away from the engine. Maintenance must inspect the aircraft before any further operation.

In my opinion, the main lesson is that ground operations are not routine paperwork. They require the same discipline as flight operations. A small mistake on the apron can create serious safety and financial consequences.

# ICAO Story — Level 5 Style

This picture shows a serious ground handling incident involving a commercial passenger aircraft and a ground support vehicle. The engine nacelle is clearly damaged, and the vehicle is positioned dangerously close to the engine, which suggests that there has been contact between the vehicle and the aircraft.

The most likely scenario is a collision during pushback, towing, or ground servicing. However, there could be several contributing factors. The vehicle driver may have misjudged the clearance, the marshaller may not have given clear signals, or the communication between the cockpit and ground crew may have broken down. The wet apron surface is also relevant because it can reduce traction and increase stopping distance for ground vehicles.

From an operational point of view, this aircraft should be considered unserviceable until a full technical inspection is completed. The visible deformation of the nacelle is only the obvious part of the problem. The real danger is hidden damage to fan blades, engine structure, sensors, wiring, or mounting points. If this aircraft departed without proper inspection, the crew could face an engine malfunction in flight, which would be much more dangerous than a delay on the ground.

The correct response should be immediate and disciplined. The crew should stop the operation, secure the aircraft, shut down the engine if it is running, and inform ground control. The ground crew should stop the vehicle, secure the area, and check for any fuel, oil, or hydraulic leakage. Maintenance engineers should perform a detailed inspection and decide whether the aircraft is airworthy.

In my opinion, this picture is a good reminder that apron safety depends on small margins. Aircraft engines are large, expensive, and vulnerable during ground operations. Ground vehicles must maintain safe distance, and all team members must use clear communication and standard procedures.

**Safety conclusion:** the aircraft must not continue the flight until maintenance clears it. It is always better to delay a flight on the ground than to discover engine damage in the air.
