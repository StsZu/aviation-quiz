STEP 1
MAKE A TOPIC STATEMENT

Start by offering a brief overview of the image, which will provide a general understanding of the situation in the scene. One or two sentences are enough!

USEFUL LANGUAGE:
The incident involves ...
This is a ...
I can see ...
There is/are ...
The picture shows ...
The picture depicts ...
This picture illustrates ...
The image portrays ...

EXAMPLE:
“This picture depicts a British Airways aircraft that has suffered an engine fire. Passengers can be seen calmly distancing themselves from the aircraft having evacuated via the emergency chutes.”

STEP 2
DESCRIBE THE AIRCRAFT

This is a great chance to show off your aviation vocabulary! Go ahead and talk about the TYPE of aircraft, any interesting FEATURES that it may have, and what CONDITION it is in, especially if your photograph shows an accident aircraft.

EXAMPLE:
“What looks like a Boeing 737 has left the runway and slid down a steep bank. The right engine has been ripped off and the the landing gear has probably collapsed. The emergency slides have been deployed and the rear and over-wing doors are open. Apart from some damage to the right wing, the fuselage looks to be mostly intact. The left engine has dug into the soft ground and this may have helped prevent the aircraft from sliding further down the hill.”

USEFUL LANGUAGE:
TYPE: Boeing, Airbus, seaplane, general aviation, commercial, firefighting, military, ...

FEATURES: swept wing, t-tail, delta wing, high/low wing, retractable landing gear, distinctive livery, wide/narrow body, twin engine, single piston engine, ...

DAMAGE: ripped off, buckled, cracked, shattered, collapsed, punctured, folded back, bent, twisted, snapped, ...

STEP 3
HYPOTHESISE

Make guesses about what you can’t see in the picture. What happened before the picture was taken? How are the people feeling now? What do you think will happen next?

EXAMPLE:
“Although the runway is not visible, this looks like a runway overshoot. The weather conditions may have been a contributory factor to the incident; it is currently quite overcast, but when the incident happened there could have been heavy rain or standing water on the runway which caused the aircraft to skid. The over-wing door is open so I guess the passengers all managed to evacuate, although it's possible some of them sustained some minor injuries. This must have been a very scary experience for the passengers.”

USEFUL LANGUAGE:
Second/Third conditionals: If I were a passenger on this plane, I would feel shaken up. If the aircraft had been going faster, it would have ended up in the water.
Modal verbs for possibility/certainty: The people in the background might be local residents who have come to have a look.
Look/seem/appear: The aircraft seems to be balancing delicately on the edge of the river bank and it looks like it could fall in at any moment.
Adverbials of probability: maybe, perhaps, certainly, definitely, probably, obviously, clearly ...

STEP 4
BACKGROUND

Look at the background of the photo. What other things do you see? Think about the terrain, airport layout, ground personnel, vehicles, equipment, airport signs, markings and lighting.

EXAMPLE:
“This airfield seems to be located in a very dry, arid area. It is surrounded by flat terrain and looks particularly sandy and stony. In the distance, some rocky mountains are visible on the horizon. The terrain seems to be completely devoid of vegetation indicating that there is probably very little rainfall in this area. In the foreground, an aircraft is connected to the jet bridge and several ground crew in high-vis clothing can be seen next to the baggage cart. A belt loader is sitting idle at the bottom of the image.”

USEFUL LANGUAGE:
Ground support vehicles/equipment: fuel truck, ground power unit (GPU), passenger/apron buses, container loader, belt loader, snow plough, de-icing vehicle, air stairs, jet bridge, pushback tug, firefighting trucks, ambulance, follow me car, baggage carts ...
People: marshaller, baggage handler/ramp agent, fire fighter, paramedic, falconer, maintenance engineer, cabin crew, air traffic controller ...
Terrain: snow-capped mountains, steep slopes, wide grasslands, harvested fields, winding river, murky lake, rolling hills, sparse vegetation, sandy dunes, dense forest, lush greenery, built up area, cityscape, skyscrapers, rural village, ...

STEP 5
WEATHER

Finally, don’t forget to mention the weather conditions, particularly if they have an impact on what is happening in the photo.

EXAMPLE:
“Although it is not snowing at the moment, there is a thick layer of snow covering the ground indicating a recent heavy snowfall. The airport is at risk of becoming snowbound and the weather conditions must be disrupting the usual flow of traffic. De-icing activities are in full swing, with ground crews busy ensuring that the aircraft wings and fuselage are clear of ice in these frigid temperatures.”

USEFUL LANGUAGE:
Snow: glistening snow, pristine, blizzard, flurries of snow, snowdrift, frosty conditions, heavy snowfall, blanketed in snow, ...
Sunny weather: clear blue skies, fair weather, bright and sunny, unobstructed view, glare, CAVOK, ...
Wind: light breeze, wind shear, blustery conditions, turbulence, crosswind, gusts, gales, ...
Rain: heavy downpour, light drizzle, drenched, cloudburst, flooding, bucketing down, driving rain, torrential rain, ...
Cloud: overcast, TCU (towering cumulus), isolated/embedded CB clouds, scattered/broken clouds, wispy cirrus clouds, gloomy, anvil clouds, mammatus clouds...
Fog: patches of fog, low/reduced visibility, misty, engulfed in fog, dense fog, ...
