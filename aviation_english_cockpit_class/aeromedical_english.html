Critical Safety Nuances

First-Aid Vocabulary Pitfalls

Using incorrect terms during an aviation emergency can cause passenger anxiety or crew confusion. Avoid these common semantic mistakes.

Severity: High

Cardiac Arrest vs. Heart Attack

Crucial medical distinction for radio relays and cabin management.

Heart Attack: Blocked blood flow to heart. Patient is usually awake, complaining of severe chest discomfort.
Cardiac Arrest: Heart stops beating completely. Patient collapses, stops breathing, and needs CPR immediately.
Severity: Moderate

To Control vs. To Stop Bleeding

Subtle differences when communicating first-aid results to the captain.

To Control Bleeding: Action of reducing blood flow from a major wound by applying active pressure.
To Stop Bleeding: Achieving full arrest of blood loss before backup emergency services arrive.
Severity: Informative

Plaster (UK) vs. Cast

Differentiating minor cut adhesive from major bone fractures.

Plaster (UK): A tiny, soft adhesive bandage for a minor scratch or split finger.
Cast: Hard medical plaster shell to immobilize a fractured arm/leg.

Master Review Checklist

Onboard Medical Checklist Summary

Never compromise safety. When reporting medical emergencies to the flight deck, always clearly state:

The patient's conscious state: Are they an incapacitated person or did they just faint temporarily?
Critical Actions Taken: E.g., administered first aid, applied pressure to control bleeding, or used an AED.
Specific conditions present: Is it a stroke, epileptic seizure, or deep vein thrombosis?
Onboard resources deployed: Note the use of specialized tools like an inhaler or EpiPen.
