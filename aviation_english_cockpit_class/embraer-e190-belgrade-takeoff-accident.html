OFFICIAL DISPATCH BRIEFING SHEET
INCIDENT DOSSIER

On February 18, 2024, an Embraer E190-200LR operated by Marathon Airlines for Air Serbia was involved in a serious takeoff performance accident at Nikola Tesla Aerodrome (LYBE) in Belgrade, Serbia.

AIRCRAFT TYPE Embraer E190-200LR
REGISTRATION OY-GDC
ATTEMPTED RUNWAY INT. Intersection D5 (Wrong)
EXPECTED RUNWAY INT. Intersection D6 (Correct)
TORA AVAILABLE (D5) 1,273 Meters
TORA REQUIRED 1,440 Meters

Accident Dynamics: The aircraft mistakenly initiated the takeoff roll from runway intersection D5 instead of D6. As a result, the crew began takeoff with only 1,273 meters of Runway TORA, which was 167 meters less than safety margins dictated.

During takeoff roll, the cockpit crew realized they were approaching the end of the runway. Despite this, the captain failed to select maximum takeoff thrust (TOGA), continuing instead with reduced FLEX thrust. The aircraft crossed the runway boundary, struck airport ground equipment (including the ILS Far Field Monitor antenna), sustaining extreme structural damage, but managed to return for an emergency landing. There were no injuries.

ICAO EXAM TASK 1: INCIDENT OVERVIEW

Prepare a structured response describing the incident. Structure your speech using the system checklist below:

  • Where & When? Location coordinates, exact weather or date factors
  • Causes? Split into direct crew errors and FMS mismatches

The accident happened on February 18, 2024, at Nikola Tesla Aerodrome in Belgrade. An Embraer E190 attempted to take off from the wrong runway intersection. The crew planned to use intersection D6, but they entered the runway at D5.

The main problem was that the available takeoff distance from D5 was too short. The aircraft needed at least 1,440 meters, but only 1,273 meters were available. The crew also failed to update the FMS correctly, and the aircraft used an incorrect flap setting and reduced thrust.

During the takeoff roll, the crew realized that the runway end was approaching, but they did not apply maximum thrust. The aircraft failed to climb properly and struck ground equipment. Fortunately, nobody was injured, but the aircraft and airport infrastructure were badly damaged. This case shows that runway verification, proper briefing, and strong crew resource management are essential for flight safety.

ICAO EXAM TASK 2: TARGETED INTERROGATION Q&A
Model Answer: The runway distance was critical because the aircraft did not have enough space to take off safely. From intersection D5, only 1,273 meters of TORA were available. However, the aircraft required at least 1,440 meters for its actual weight and configuration. This created a dangerous situation because the aircraft could not accelerate and climb normally. In aviation, runway length is not flexible; it is a hard safety limit.
Model Answer: Crew Resource Management played a major role in this accident. The captain made unsafe decisions, but the first officer did not challenge them strongly enough. Communication between the pilots was not effective, and standard procedures were not followed. In a safe cockpit, both pilots must monitor each other and speak up when something is wrong. This accident shows that CRM is not just theory; it can prevent real accidents.
Model Answer: FMS verification is important because the aircraft must be configured according to the correct runway, weight, thrust, and flap settings. If the FMS contains incorrect data, the crew may believe the takeoff is safe when it is not. In this case, the captain changed some data but did not properly update the FMS configuration. This created a mismatch between the required performance and the actual aircraft configuration. Before takeoff, pilots must make sure that the FMS agrees with the real situation.
Model Answer: The safest option would have been to stop and clarify the situation with air traffic control. The crew should have recalculated the performance carefully and verified the runway data, FMS, thrust setting, and flap setting. They should not have rushed the decision. If there was any doubt about the available runway distance, they should have rejected the takeoff plan and requested repositioning. In aviation, taking extra time is much safer than continuing with uncertain data.
