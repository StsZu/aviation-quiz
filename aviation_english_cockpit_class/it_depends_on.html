Core Operational Definition

ATC-DOC-01
“ “It depends on” means:
The operational answer changes dynamically based on critical situational parameters.

As a pilot, giving a strict "yes" or "no" is often a safety hazard. Proving to the examiner that you calculate multiple factors showcases maturity and strong situational assessment.

BASIC SYNTAX RULE:
It depends on + noun / noun phrase.

Strong ICAO Sentence Blueprint

By deploying the following three-step structural flow, you demonstrate complete linguistic control under situational stress:

STEP 1: DEFINE CONDITIONS "It depends on [factor 1], [factor 2], and [factor 3]."
STEP 2: DEFINE DECISION "If [condition], the pilot should [action]."
STEP 3: PRIORITIZE SAFETY "The main priority is to maintain safety."
ACTIVE FORMULA SAMPLE:

"It depends on the weather, runway condition, and aircraft performance. If the approach is unstable, the pilot should go around. The main priority is to maintain safety."

Warning Checklist (Common Errors)

Avoid critical translation crash landings. Review these structural comparisons from actual pilot examinations:

CRITICAL ERROR CORRECT FLIGHT VERSION TECHNICAL REASON
"It depends from weather." "It depends on the weather." Preposition violation. Always pair depend with on.
"It depends of situation." "It depends on the situation." Unacceptable direct translation. Use "on".
"Depends on weather." "It depends on the weather." Grammar fragment. Examiners require full semantic clauses in assessments.
"It depends on how is weather." "It depends on what the weather is like." Incorrect word order within nested interrogative clauses.
"It depends on pilot." "It depends on the pilot’s experience." Too vague. Precise terminology defines high-score outputs.

FULL FLIGHT BRIEFING SPEECH ENGINE

"A pilot’s decision depends on many factors. It depends on the weather, aircraft condition, fuel quantity, air traffic control instructions, and the overall safety margin.

For example, if the weather at the destination becomes worse, the pilot may decide to continue, hold, or divert to another airport. This decision depends on visibility, wind, fuel remaining, and alternate airports.

In an emergency, the decision also depends on altitude, time available, and the seriousness of the problem. The pilot must first maintain control of the aircraft and then choose the safest option.

In aviation, good decisions are not based on emotion. They depend on facts, procedures, and safety."
