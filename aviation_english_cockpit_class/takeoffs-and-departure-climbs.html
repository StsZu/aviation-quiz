03 / LEXICON FAA RECOGNIZED STANDARDS

Technical Vocabulary

Ground Effect AERODYNAMICS
A condition of improved performance encountered up to an altitude of one wingspan above the surface, characterized by reduced wingtip vortices and a drop in induced drag.
$V_X$ (Best Angle of Climb) PERFORMANCE
The airspeed that yields the greatest gain in altitude for a given distance over the ground, crucial for clearing obstacles.
$V_Y$ (Best Rate of Climb) PERFORMANCE
The airspeed that yields the greatest gain in altitude per unit of time, used for standard departure climbs.
Weathervaning CROSSWIND
The aerodynamic tendency of an aircraft on the ground to turn its nose directly into the relative wind.
P-Factor & Torque PROPULSION
Asymmetric propeller loading and engine rotational torque that pull the nose of the aircraft to the left during acceleration.
Skipping / Side-skipping STRESSES
A series of small bounces and sideways movements during crosswind takeoffs that impose severe side stresses on the landing gear.

06 / DRILL ORAL FLUENCY WORKSHOP

Speaking Prompts

SCENARIO: GROUND EFFECT TRAP
Explain why an airplane might be able to lift off but unable to climb under high density altitude conditions.
"In ground effect, induced drag is reduced by up to 50 percent close to the runway surface. This allows the airplane to lift off below the normal flight speed. However, as it climbs out of ground effect (above one wingspan), drag increases sharply. Under high density altitude, high temperature, or maximum gross weight, the engine lacks the performance to overcome this extra drag, making the aircraft unable to climb out."
SCENARIO: CROSSWIND LIFT-OFF
What is the correct wing and wheel handling sequence as the nose-wheel raises during a strong crosswind takeoff?
"As the nose-wheel raises, the pilot must hold aileron pressure into the wind. This prevents the upwind wing from lifting. Consequently, the downwind wing will rise and the downwind main wheel will lift off first, allowing the aircraft to finish its takeoff roll on the single upwind main wheel. This avoids side-skipping and prevents structural damage."
SCENARIO: ENGINE FAILURE AT DEPARTURE
Describe the immediate corrective actions of a pilot experiencing engine failure shortly after lift-off.
"The pilot must immediately lower the nose to prevent an aerodynamic stall, as the aircraft is at a high climb attitude. The pilot should coordinate rudder input to counteract any yaw (since right rudder was previously held) and establish a controlled glide straight ahead. A turn back to the runway should only be attempted if trained and sufficient altitude exists."

08 / FINAL REVISION DEPARTURE FLOW BRIEFING

Flight Dispatch Checklist

NORMAL TO DEPARTURE CLIMB
  • Line up aligned to centerline. Choose far tracking references.
  • Advance throttle continuously to counteract left-turning factors.
  • Rotate cleanly to attitude corresponding to $V_Y$.
  • Maintain climb power until reaching at least 500 feet AGL.
MAX PERFORMANCE / RECOVERY
  • Short-field: Rotate at $V_X$ and maintain until obstacles are cleared.
  • Soft-field: Raise nose-wheel as early as possible. Level off in ground effect.
  • Engine failure: Lower nose immediately, level wings, glide straight.
  • Noise Abatement: Review Chart Supplements before start.
