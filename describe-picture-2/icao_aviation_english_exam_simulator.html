The ICAO 5-Step Structural Framework

Every pilot must break down any incident, turnaround, or operational hazard in an organized sequence to demonstrate immediate level-5 communication discipline.

STEP 01

Topic Statement

Deliver a high-impact overview of the visual moment or occurrence instantly.

STEP 02

Aircraft Details

Name the type, configuration, engines, livery, and active external hardware settings.

STEP 03

Hypothesise

Deduce immediate triggers, historical variables, structural stresses, or crew stress levels.

STEP 04

Background context

Explain physical geography, airfield layout features, emergency assets, or natural terrain.

STEP 05

Weather Status

Determine cloud density, precipitation risks, turbulence, visibility constraints, or wind direction.

MUST-KNOW LINGUISTICS

Tactical Modal Phrasing

Evaluators actively scan your speech for structure markers expressing certainty, possibility, indicators, safety assessments, and recommended operational decisions.

Level 4 vs. Level 5 Distinction: Overusing generic modal verbs like "maybe it was..." limits your score. Level 5 candidates use precise phrasing like "may indicate a..." or "must have been a..." to express varying levels of probability.
Strong Certainty

must have been

"It must have been a routine turnaround."

Used to establish high-probability logical deductions.

Possibility / Conjecture

could / might have been

"It could have been caused by an unstable approach."

Ideal for stating technical hypotheses without direct evidence.

Physical Clues

may indicate / suggest

"The heavy spray may indicate a wet surface."

Bridges observable visual elements to theoretical states.

Operational Safety

From a safety standpoint

"From a safety perspective, rapid evacuation is vital."

Signals professional decision-making priorities instantly.

EXAM CRITERIA METRICS

ICAO Evaluation Safety Warnings

Review common grammatical pitfalls and see how minor visual and structure adjustments can transform average descriptions into high-scoring safety statements.

TOPIC STATEMENT AVOID VAGUE TEXT
"The plane is landing and hitting its nose wheel."
"The picture shows a twin-engined turboprop landing nose-low with the nose wheel hitting the runway first."

Correction logic: Introduce the precise propulsion system (twin-engined turboprop) and describe spatial attitude (nose-low landing) for precision.

SPECULATION AVOID WEAK MODALS
"Maybe it was because of wind."
"This could have been caused by an unstable approach or a sudden localized gust."

Correction logic: Avoid basic terms like 'maybe'. Instead, use passive past modals (could have been caused) paired with professional vocabulary (unstable approach, gust).

SAFETY CONCLUSION AVOID BASIC ADVICE
"They must stop the plane so they don't break it."
"From a safety standpoint, the safest decision would be to abandon the landing and initiate a go-around."

Correction logic: Frame safety around high-scoring idioms (From a safety standpoint/safest decision would be) and standard maneuvers (go-around).

Active Speaking Drill Strategy

The fastest route to high-level certification is repeating the standard descriptions aloud until your rhythm is automatic. Focus on maintaining a steady flow with professional modal structures instead of over-analyzing visual details.
