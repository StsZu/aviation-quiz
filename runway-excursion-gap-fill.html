Alright, so in this photo I see a narrow body aircraft, possibly a Boeing 737 that is partially submerged in the water. This is clearly the result of a runway excursion since I can see part of the runway in the background and the tire tracks leading to the water.

It looks like the airplane was safely evacuated because I can see at least two emergency evacuation slides that were deployed. There’s an orange line surrounding this airplane which is most likely there to prevent environmental damage by containing any debris or hazardous fluids from the aircraft.

I see three or four people, possibly accident investigators standing on the right wing of the aircraft and it looks like one of them is stepping out of the emergency exit door. They probably traveled from the shoreline to the aircraft using that yellow raft that’s sitting just below the leading edge of the wing.

Standing on the shoreline behind the aircraft are a few more people. Some of them are wearing hard hats and reflective clothing so they may be accident investigators or news reporters. There are also a few trucks and vehicles parked on the grass and on the runway including two fire rescue vehicles on the upper left side of the photo.

I don’t really see a sense of urgency among the individuals near and around the airplane so I think this runway excursion happened well before this photo was taken. It also probably took a decent amount of time to set up those orange cones around the runway edge and to remove that radome from the fuselage.

The flood light at the upper right hand corner of this photo makes me believe this event occurred at night.

So, what could’ve caused this accident? Well, like many accidents there’s usually no single cause but rather a number of contributing factors. I can’t tell if this occurred during takeoff or landing but the aircraft may have encountered some type of windshear or convective activity.

If so, the pilots may have encountered stiff crosswinds or a tailwind which would’ve greatly increased the stopping distance on the runway. If storms were over the airfield, then the water on the runway from the rain would’ve certainly decreased the braking performance of the aircraft.

And speaking of braking performance, it’s also possible that one of the braking mechanisms malfunctioned, such as the wheel brakes, speed brakes or thrust reversers. We can’t see if the thrust reversers were deployed in this photo since the engines are submerged but it’s clear that the speed brakes are not deployed.

But they may have been retracted before the aircraft was evacuated.
